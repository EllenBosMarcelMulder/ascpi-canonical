## ABSTRACT

Using ASCPI Native Language, an operating system is defined as a canonical module M = (Φ, Σ, Δ), where system lifecycle, submodule relations, and structural constraints are represented exclusively through finite phase spaces, mechanically derivable state, and phase-based transition laws.

A minimal proof system is constructed consisting of two canonical modules—ASCpiOS_Canon and WindowManager_Canon—validated through binary invariant checks and loaded into a constrained runtime enforcing fixed topology and canonical time progression.

Crucially, this work demonstrates that **structural completeness, rather than behavioral implementation**, is sufficient for the self-description of complex computational systems. The proof is intentionally frozen at the moment of canonical closure to preserve formal integrity and prevent semantic drift.

This establishes ASCPI as a viable foundation for declarative system specification, canonical architecture proofs, and future research into semantic-free computing paradigms.
